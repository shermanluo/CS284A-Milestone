1 INTRODUCTION
This article describes our current progress so far on our CS184/CS184A project. Our project goal is to implement a fast
and ecient method for the shattering of brittle objects as in [
1
]. Our stretch goals are to optimize the algorithm by
implementing modications involving making our shattering algorithm hierarchical with respect to the primitives and
also making our shattering more realistic by exploring better crack propagation frameworks.
2 PROGRESS
We rst wanted to decide among us a general timeline of modular steps required to accomplish our goals. This was the
rst thing we did, and our goal was to make the steps as modular as possible so we could split up the tasks eciently.
Ultimately everything below is included in a detailed and organized design document.
2.1 Action Plan
•
Find a way to represent solid brittle objects, such as a vase, as a set of tetrahedrons with a point mass within that
have limited-force constraints that keep neighboring point masses attached.
•
The tetrahedron model of an object is simulated to behave as a single rigid object instead of complicated dynamics
between every point mass. We need to nd an animation framework that will allow us to import our models and
simulate the dynamics of our solid objects over time that can vary forces, time-steps, velocities, accelerations,
etc., and can integrate these over time to produce a nice looking animation.
•
Model the way our object receives some sort of impact, characterized as an impulse distribution on the point
masses over time, usually just on a small number of points.

2.2 Implementation and Frameworks
After some research and exploration, we have chosen to simulate our shattering in Blender (step 2) and to generate our
models in TetGen (step 1). Though we cannot show results of our shattering just yet, we can show simple animation of
objects, and generation of our tetrahedronalized objects in Blender.
